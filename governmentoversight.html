Poor Government Oversight

Frequent government deceit and lies fueled the crisis.

  • Summary
  • Sources

Summary

State officials insisted that the water was safe to drink after the pipelines were switched. Even though officials received many complaints and reports about water quality, they continued to turn a blind eye towards the situation and assured the public that the water was safe to drink. It wasn’t until January 6th, 2016 Governor Snyder declared a state of emergency and the Department of Justice launched an investigation into the crisis a year after residents were exposed to the dirty water.

The Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) failed their oversight responsibilities in all levels of government--local, state, and federal. The EPA did not carry out their duties under the Safe Drinking Water Act, the Michigan state environmental agency was responsible for a delayed crisis response that allowed Flint residents to become more exposed to lead, and the city at a lower level didn’t abide by preventative rules for lead exposure.

An independent task conducted an investigation and found criminal negligence at many levels of the government in producing unauthentic water quality reports. This ultimately caused more Flint residents to get exposed to lead contaminated water for a longer time. Ultimately, after 12 months of grand jury, 9 people were indicted on criminal charges related to actions or a lack thereof that contributed to the historic injustice of the Flint water crisis.

The city of Flint was already distressed before the Flint water crisis was initiated, thus exacerbating the previous problems the city was facing. The Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) provided response and recovery assistance for the crisis by connecting community members to helpful resources, facilitating blood lead screening procedures, and evaluating children lead exposure and its consequences. More importantly, the CDC’s response to the crisis highlighted the need for increased risk communication among government members and agencies, the lack of quality public infrastructure, and lack of primary prevention efforts.
